Product work splits three ways: mechanical, interpretive, and proactive.

The **mechanical** stuff (fix the typo, add the index, flip the flag) is work a computer can do better than you. The **proactive** stuff – deciding what to build next – requires context, judgement, and that other word everyone won't stop saying: [taste](/newsletter/good-taste-great-products).

The interesting lane is **interpretive**: incidents, customer issues, funnel drops. Work a computer *could* do, but only with the right tools and permissions. That's where self-driving products live.

Today, [PostHog Code](/code) enters beta. It's a desktop app that orchestrates coding agents on top of your product data – automating the mechanical work, and surfacing the interpretive work as a prioritized to-do list for you to steer.

## Borrowing a taxonomy from the car people

The Society of Automotive Engineers has a framework for self-driving. Six levels of autonomy, from Level 0 (you do everything) to Level 5 (you can take a nap). It's a good mental model because it forces specificity about where the machine takes over and where a human is still on the hook.

This maps surprisingly well onto building products with AI:

**Level 0 – No automation.** Analytics without AI, code without autocomplete. Dashboards, filters, SQL. Really good docs are your best bet for troubleshooting. Engineers write all the code, frame every question, and interpret all the data themselves. This was most of what shipped before 2024.

**Level 1 – Engineer assistance.** The agent answers specific, well-formed questions like *"How many users are on the pro plan?"* and drafts short, scoped snippets when you point at the file. It falls apart when the question is ambiguous or the change spans more than a function. Engineers still decide what to ask, where to put the code, and how to read the answer. This was the first version of [PostHog AI](/ai).

**Level 2 – Partial automation.** The agent handles ambiguity, stitches queries together, and writes working functions and files on demand. It answers vague, loaded questions like *"Which features do users engage with most?"* without constant handholding. Engineers still have to know what questions to ask, and how to interpret the output. PostHog AI [reached this state in 2025](/blog/8-learnings-from-1-year-of-agents-posthog-ai), once reasoning models matured.

**Level 3 – Conditional automation.** The agent compresses massive amounts of context and responds not just to the query, but suggests meaningful follow-up actions – often as a draft PR. It summarizes thousands of session recordings *and* proposes the change that addresses them. Engineers review rather than originate; interpretation starts migrating from human to agent. PostHog AI crossed into this territory in late 2025.

**Level 4 – High automation.** The agent understands your codebase, your product data, and the surrounding context – support tickets, internal comms, industry news. It proposes and ships work without being asked *(and is trusted to read, write, and delete)*. The [PostHog MCP](/docs/model-context-protocol) enables a working prototype of this today. Engineers set guardrails and approve outcomes; the agent does most of the framing and interpretation.

**Level 5 – Full automation.** The full product autonomy loop runs on its own. Signals come in, the agent decides what to do, ships the change, evaluates whether it worked, and updates its memory for next time. You still weigh in on strategy and direction, but the loop between *what users are doing* and *what ships to fix or extend it* closes without a human turning the crank.


## Why vibe-coding tools plateau at Level 3
A self-driving car isn't just a vehicle that gets itself where you point it (that's cruise control and lane assist). Similarly, a self-driving product isn't just a really good coding agent. To reach level 5, a product must have a concept of where it is, what's around it, what those things might do next, and what it will do in response. Once it executes a decision, it must evaluate whether the execution matched the plan, and adjust the next decision based on what it learned.

Critically, it does all of that continuously, without being asked. The *self* in self-driving isn't autonomy from the engineer. It's autonomy from instruction.

Vibecoding tools aren't aiming for autonomy from instruction. They're aiming for speed per instruction – and they've gotten *very* good at it. Apps like Lovable, Replit and [v0](/blog/posthog-v0-integration) have proven you can get surprisingly far by equipping agents with tools and skills: plug in the right MCP servers, point the agent at a goal, ship a working app before lunch. That's a legitimate Level 3 experience for *building* software, and it's why [AI has quietly eaten the no-code category](/blog/ai-is-killing-no-code-experiments).

The problem is what happens after lunch. In a typical week, PostHog customers generate 100,000+ failed queries, 1.2 to 2.6 million new error-tracking issues, and 12 to 47 million raw exceptions. Each one is a candidate signal for an agent to act on – and none of it is visible to a tool whose worldview ends at the last git push. 

To shift out of cruise control and into self-driving, an agentic engineering tool needs five things: **tools, skills, signals, memory,** and **evaluation**. Our [AI engineering handbook](/handbook/engineering/ai/ai-platform) goes deep on each one. Here's the quick version.

### 1. Tools – atomic capabilities

Tools are agent-friendly descriptions of what your product can do. At PostHog we treat them as [atomic capabilities](/handbook/engineering/ai/implementation#mcp-tools-vs-skills) – CRUD operations and simple actions like `Create_insight`. One tool in particular does disproportionate work for coding agents: `read_taxonomy`. It lets the agent check what events and properties actually exist before it writes a query or an instrumentation PR. 

### 2. Skills – how to get the job done

If tools are the fork and knife, skills are the recipe. A [skill](/handbook/engineering/ai/writing-skills) combines tools, resources, and constraints into a playbook. The clearest evidence of a skills gap is in our own data: the single most-called tool on PostHog's MCP server is `docs-search`, at ~28K calls a month. That's every coding agent (more or less), asking our docs for directions. PostHog Code has skills for the engineering workflows we see most often – instrumenting events, auditing feature flags, adding error tracking. Writing a skill feels like writing a doc – and [most engineers would rather ship the feature](/newsletter/agent-first-product-engineering) than document it. But for a non-human user, a skill *is* the feature.

### 3. Signals – when work should happen

Tools and skills guide the *what*. Signals determine the *when*. PostHog Code sits directly on the signal source: your product data. Instead of you noticing a pattern and prompting the agent, the pattern itself is the prompt. Raw observations get deduplicated, contextualized, and turned into concrete plans the agent can execute or queue for your review. You stop triaging a noisy inbox and start opening a pre-framed to-do list.

### 4. Memory – what the agent already knows

Signals tell the agent something is happening *now*. Memory tells it what happened last time (and whether you yelled at it yesterday for em dashes instead of [en dashes](/handbook/content/posthog-style-guide#use-british-style-en-dashes)). Without memory, the agent is a goldfish with git access – re-opening the same PR every Tuesday because the same signal fires every Tuesday. It also keeps costs sane; we [caught our own AI agent embezzling tokens](/blog/optimizing-agent-cost) because it kept re-running work it had already done.

### 5. Evaluation – did the thing actually work?

The loop doesn't close without this. [Testing AI agents](/blog/testing-ai-agents) looks nothing like testing traditional software, and "it runs without erroring" is not a pass condition. PostHog Code schedules evaluation as a long-running Temporal job, so the check runs hours or days after a PR merges. The same dashboard, funnel, experiment, or [LLM-as-a-judge eval](/blog/stop-ai-slop) that fired the signal gets re-queried, and if the metric didn't move (or moved the wrong way), the agent reverts or reopens the work. Skip this step and you don't have a self-driving product – you have a PR generator with opinions.

## The product autonomy loop

Stitch the five together and a pattern falls out:

*Collect data → cluster signals → check memory → notify workers → do work → evaluate → write back to memory.*

That's the product autonomy loop. Tools, skills, and signals get you to Level 4. Memory and evaluation are what Level 5 is built out of. You can't reliably do this inside a general-purpose coding agent, because the signals, memory, and evaluation data all live somewhere else. For a lot of companies, that somewhere else is PostHog.

## Why we built PostHog Code (and what that means for you)

We've spent years building a weirdly complete stack for this loop to actually work:

- **Data** – years of it. [Errors](/docs/error-tracking/start-here), [session replays](/docs/session-replay/start-here), [LLM traces](/docs/llm-analytics/start-here), [experiments](/docs/experiments/start-here), [feature flags](/docs/feature-flags/start-here), revenue. The event stream of what real users are actually doing.
- **Warehouse** – your full data stack in one place: product events, application data, and anything you sync from Stripe, Salesforce, Snowflake, BigQuery, or flat files. Runs on ClickHouse today, DuckDB in the near future.
- **Orchestration** – Temporal for long-running jobs that outlive any single agent session, so evaluation runs hours after execution without anyone holding the process open.
- **Sandboxes** – cloud agents (recently added) to run code safely outside of production.
- **Devtools** – a growing catalog of the things product engineers actually reach for.

PostHog Code is the interface that connects all of it. It sits on top of your product data and does two things:

**First, it ships PRs automatically for mechanical decisions.** Something obvious breaks, the agent pulls in context, writes the fix, and schedules an evaluation job. Cost of getting it slightly wrong: a one-line revert, auto-triggered if the metric moves the wrong way.

**Second, it frames bounded decisions for you to act on.** Ten support tickets about a confusing checkout flow? PostHog Code checks the replays, quantifies the drop-off, and drafts a PR for your to iterate, kill, or deploy.

You can switch between Claude Code and Codex from a single interface, plug in your MCP servers (Attio, Canva, Atlassian, Notion, Monday), and pull context from Linear, Zendesk, and GitHub into the same pipeline as your product data. The result is a prioritized to-do list instead of a noisy one, and automatic code changes for stuff that doesn't need your energy.

Product engineers [keep telling us this is the missing piece](/newsletter/agent-first-product-engineering) – an agent that understands not just your codebase, but your product as a whole. We're not at Level 5 yet. But every week the gap gets smaller, and you get to watch it happen from the driver's seat.
