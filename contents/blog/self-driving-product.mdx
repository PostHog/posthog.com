Product work splits two ways: reactive and proactive.

Reactive work is mechanical. Fix the typo. Add the index. Flip the flag. The data tells you what's wrong and the scope tells you what to fix. A computer can do this better than you. Full stop.

Proactive work is what happens when you prompt yourself – when you shift out of maintenance mode and decide what to build next. It needs context, judgement, and that other word everyone won't stop saying: taste.

There's a third category in between, and it's the one getting interesting: proactive work a computer could do if it had the tools, context, and permissions. That's where self-driving products live, and the lane we're launching a new product into.

Today, [PostHog Code](/code) enters beta. It's a desktop app for agentic engineering that automates the reactive parts of product building and frames the proactive work for you to steer. It's also the first public piece of a much bigger bet (more on that later).

If you've been following [our AI work](/blog/8-learnings-from-1-year-of-agents-posthog-ai), you probably saw this coming. If not, you might reasonably wonder why we think we can win in a space that has Cursor, Claude Code, Codex, and a new entrant every other Tuesday. This post is the map, how we're thinking about it, and why I think you should give our version of agentic engineering a shot.

## Borrowing a taxonomy from the car people

The Society of Automotive Engineers has had a framework for self-driving since 2014. Six levels of autonomy, from Level 0 (you do everything) to Level 5 (the car does everything, everywhere, in any condition). It's a good mental model because it forces specificity about where the machine takes over and where a human is still on the hook.

It maps surprisingly cleanly onto building products with AI.

**Level 0 – No automation.** Analytics without AI. Dashboards, filters, SQL. Everything you do, you do yourself through an interface. Really good docs are your best bet for troubleshooting. This is most of what shipped before 2024.

**Level 1 – Basic data analysis.** The agent answers specific, well-formed questions like *"How many users are on the pro plan?"* or *"What's our conversion rate?"* It falls apart when the question is ambiguous, or the query engine can't express it. This was the first version of [PostHog AI](/ai).

**Level 2 – Versatile data analysis.** The agent handles ambiguity and stitches queries together. It answers vague, loaded questions like *"Which features do users engage with most?"* PostHog AI [reached this state in 2025](/blog/8-learnings-from-1-year-of-agents-posthog-ai), once reasoning models matured and we learned how to harness them.

**Level 3 – Actionable product guidance.** The agent compresses massive amounts of context and responds not just to the query, but suggests meaningful follow-up actions. Late 2025 is when PostHog AI started to feel like the future instead of a fancy search bar – it could summarize thousands of session recordings *and* give excellent suggestions for what to do next.

**Level 4 – Proactive product maintenance.** The agent understands your codebase, your product data, and the surrounding context – support tickets, internal comms, industry news. It proposes work without being asked and has permissions to ship autonomously. The [PostHog MCP](/docs/model-context-protocol) enables a working prototype of this. You can hand product data to your agent and automate scoped work like bug fixes.

**Level 5 – Self-driving development.** An agentic system that decides, orchestrates, and evaluates itself. You might prompt work and weigh in on decisions, but it's largely self-sufficient. *"Just build me an app that generates $1M MRR, bro"* is a plausible starting prompt. That's not why you became a product engineer, though – so what does self-driving actually mean for the human in the loop?

## Code generation ≠ building software

Allow me to dodge that question for a second and make a clear distinction. A self-driving car isn't just a vehicle that gets itself where you point it (that's cruise control and lane assist).

An autonomous vehicle must know where it is, what's around it, what those things might do next, and what it will do in response. Once it executes a decision, it must evaluate whether the execution matched the plan, and adjust the next decision based on what it learned.

Critically, it does all of that continuously, without being asked. The *self* in self-driving isn't autonomy from the passenger. It's autonomy from instruction.

Apply the same variables to software and the framework looks similar:

- **Localization** – it knows what state it's in. Which users are doing what, which features are healthy, which experiments are running, what shipped yesterday.
- **Perception** – it curates its own context, from in and outside the system. Support tickets connect to session replays; Slack threads connect to code changes.
- **Prediction** – it has a working model of what's about to happen. *This cohort is about to churn. This new feature would go viral. Competitors are zigging, so we should zag.*
- **Planning** – it decides what to do based on data, and forms a plan before merging. Build a fix, run an experiment, add a button, write a doc. It also decides what *not* to do, which is equally important.
- **Control** – it has what it needs to act without asking. Skills to implement feature flags, permission to merge into master.
- **Feedback** – it checks whether the execution worked. Did engagement go up? Did latency drop? Did adding a tooltip have a meaningful impact?
- **Learning** – most importantly, it updates the next decision based on that feedback. If the fix doesn't work, it tries something else. If the experiment was conclusive, it rolls out the best variant. If you told it not to use em dashes, it actually listens to you.

## Why vibe-coding tools plateau at Level 3

Lovable, Replit, Bolt, v0, and the rest of the vibe-coding cohort have proven you can get surprisingly far by equipping agents with tools and skills. Plug in the right MCP servers, point the agent at a goal, ship a working app before lunch. That's a legitimate Level 3 experience for *building* software, and it's why [AI has quietly eaten the no-code category](/blog/ai-is-killing-no-code-experiments).

Lovable itself is already one of the top clients hitting PostHog's MCP server, and [PostHog is a first-class integration in Vercel's v0](/blog/posthog-v0-integration). The vibe-coding cohort isn't the competition here – they're customers reaching for product data to do the Level 3 job better. The ceiling they can't cross is the one that sits above that: the proactive work that starts *after* the app ships. In a typical week, PostHog customers generate 100,000+ failed queries, 1.2 to 2.6 million new error-tracking issues, and 12 to 47 million raw exceptions. Each one is a candidate signal for an agent to act on – and none of it is visible to a tool whose worldview ends at the last git push.

To shift out of cruise control and into self-driving, an agentic engineering tool needs five things: **tools, skills, signals, memory,** and **evaluation**. Our [AI engineering handbook](/handbook/engineering/ai/ai-platform) goes deep on each one. Here's the quick version.

### 1. Tools – atomic capabilities

Tools are agent-friendly descriptions of what your product can do. At PostHog, we treat them as [atomic capabilities](/handbook/engineering/ai/implementation#mcp-tools-vs-skills) – CRUD operations and simple actions that answer *"what can I do?"* Create an insight. Execute a query. Read the taxonomy. Flip a flag. Agents are good at composing simple tools; they're bad at guessing which composite tool you secretly wish you'd built.

One atomic tool in particular is doing disproportionate work at PostHog: `read_taxonomy`. It lets the agent check what events and properties actually exist before it writes a query. No `read_taxonomy`, no [hallucination protection](/handbook/engineering/ai/architecture).

MCP tool calls against our server have grown ~21× in five months – 60K in October 2025 to 1.26M in March 2026, across 70+ clients including Claude Code, Cursor, VS Code, Codex, Lovable, Windsurf, Copilot, and Gemini CLI. If you haven't shipped an MCP server or a machine-readable API surface, you're invisible in this stack. Tools are table stakes.

### 2. Skills – how to get the job done

If tools are the fork and knife, skills are the recipe. A [skill](/handbook/engineering/ai/implementation#mcp-tools-vs-skills) combines tools, query patterns, domain knowledge, and constraints into a playbook. Not a rigid script – more like a doc you'd hand a sharp new hire: *"When investigating a drop in conversion, run a funnel first, then check for correlated flag changes with `feature-flag-get-all`, then pull session replays for the drop-off step."*

The clearest evidence of a skills gap is in our own data. The single most-called tool on PostHog's MCP server is `docs-search`, at ~28K calls a month. That's every agent, more or less, asking our docs for directions. Writing a skill feels like writing a doc – and [most engineers would rather ship the feature](/newsletter/agent-first-product-engineering) than document it. But for a non-human user, a skill *is* the feature.

### 3. Signals – when work should happen

Tools and skills guide the *what*. Signals determine the *when*.

An exception spike, a checkout funnel drop, a replay of someone rage-clicking the same button three times, a cluster of support tickets that sound suspiciously alike – these are signals. Most products have tools. A few have skills. Almost nobody has signals. The [PostHog AI architecture](/handbook/engineering/ai/ai-platform#vision-product-autonomy) treats signals as the entry point to the autonomy loop: raw observations get deduplicated, contextualized, and turned into concrete plans an agent can execute.

### 4. Memory – what the agent already knows

Signals tell the agent something is happening *now*. Memory tells it what happened last time, and the time before that, and whether what it's about to try has already failed.

Without memory, the agent is a goldfish with git access. It re-opens the same PR every Tuesday because the same signal fires every Tuesday. Memory also keeps costs sane – we [caught our own AI agent embezzling tokens](/blog/optimizing-agent-cost) because it kept re-running work it had already done. Durable state prevents that.

Memory isn't a context window. It's a persistent model of the product's own history: every PR the agent has opened, linked to the signal that triggered it, linked to the metric that confirmed or denied the fix, queryable by the next agent run. PostHog's [single-loop architecture](/handbook/engineering/ai/architecture) is our version of this – the agent maintains full context across decisions instead of handing off to amnesiac sub-agents. Nearly every other coding agent on the market still skips durable memory in favor of a context window that resets the moment you start a new session.

### 5. Evaluation – did the thing actually work?

The loop doesn't close without this. An agent can ship a PR. A self-driving product ships a PR and then checks whether the thing it was trying to fix actually got fixed – against the same signal that triggered the work in the first place.

Evals are the hard part nobody was trained for. [Testing AI agents](/blog/testing-ai-agents) looks nothing like testing traditional software, and "it runs without erroring" is not a pass condition. We've written about running [LLM-as-a-judge evals](/blog/stop-ai-slop) against your own agent output for exactly this reason. At PostHog, evaluation is the last leg of the loop: a long-running job that re-queries the same dashboard, funnel, or experiment that fired the signal, and decides whether the PR moved the needle or needs a revert.

## The loop

Stitch the five together and a pattern falls out:

*Collect data → cluster signals → check memory → notify workers → do work → evaluate → write back to memory.*

That's the product autonomy loop. Tools, skills, and signals get you to Level 4. Memory and evaluation are what Level 5 is built out of. You can't reliably do this inside a general-purpose coding agent, because the signals, memory, and evaluation data all live somewhere else. For a lot of companies, that somewhere else is PostHog.

Which brings us back to Level 5 – and the question I ducked earlier.

## Why we built PostHog Code (and what that means for you)

We've been sitting on a weirdly complete set of ingredients for this loop:

- **Data** – years of it. [Errors](/docs/error-tracking/start-here), [session replays](/docs/session-replay/start-here), [LLM traces](/docs/llm-analytics/start-here), [experiments](/docs/experiments/start-here), [feature flags](/docs/feature-flags/start-here), revenue. The event stream of what real users are actually doing.
- **Warehouse** – ClickHouse for storing and querying events and agent decision history in the same place.
- **Orchestration** – Temporal for long-running jobs that outlive any single agent session, so evaluation runs hours after execution without anyone holding the process open.
- **Sandboxes** – cloud agents for running code safely, without blowing up production.
- **Devtools** – a growing catalog of the things product engineers actually reach for.

PostHog Code is the first public piece. It sits on top of your product data and does two things.

**First, it ships PRs automatically for mechanical decisions.** Something obvious breaks, the agent pulls in context, writes the fix, and schedules an evaluation job. Cost of getting it slightly wrong: a one-line revert, auto-triggered if the metric moves the wrong way.

**Second, it frames bounded decisions so you can actually decide.** Ten support tickets about a confusing checkout flow? The agent checks the replays, quantifies the drop-off, checks memory for whether this was already discussed in a previous run, and drafts the options. You still make the call – redesign, kill, or ignore. The legwork is already done.

The same pattern plays out for the internal agents we've already built ourselves, like [the one that writes Andy's standup notes for him](/blog/standup-bot-revenge). The difference with PostHog Code is that you don't have to be an AI-pilled engineer to wire one up.

Practically, that means a prioritized to-do list instead of a noisy one, and automatic code changes for stuff that doesn't need your energy. You can switch between Claude Code and Codex from a single interface, plug in your MCP servers (Attio, Canva, Atlassian, Notion, Monday), and pull context from Linear, Zendesk, and GitHub into the same pipeline as your product data.

## The TL;DR (and CTA)

Our long-term vision is Level 5 self-driving. We're not *quite* there yet. The current version of PostHog Code is more of a workbench. It's useful on its own, and – more importantly – it's ahead of general-purpose agents on the one axis we care about: **product context comes pre-loaded.**

You don't have to tab-switch, copy-paste, or upload a screenshot of your own product into the agent chat like a lunatic. PostHog Code runs in the background, watches your data, fixes what's fixable, and frames what needs a judgement call. Product engineers [keep telling us this is the missing piece](/newsletter/agent-first-product-engineering) – an agent that sees what real users do, not just what your IDE sees.

A workbench isn't the destination. It's the shop we're building the vehicle in. Every week, the set of decisions we can safely automate gets wider. Skills get better. Data gets richer. Feedback loops get tighter.

The human stays in the seat for taste, strategy, and the freedom to build what data can't yet dictate. The agent handles everything downstream of that.
