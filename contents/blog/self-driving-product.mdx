Product work typically splits two ways: reactive and proactive.

Reactive work is mechanical (fix the typo, add the index, flip the flag). The data tells you what's wrong; the scope tells you what to fix. Cursor, Claude Code, and Codex already play this game exceptionally well.

Then there's innovation you prompt yourself. No alarms are going off, nobody's asking — but you have ideas about what to build next. Proactive work needs context, and more importantly, ownership. And let's not forget Silicon Valley's favorite new buzzwords: judgement and taste.

There's a third category in between, growing in importance: proactive work an agent *could* do — if it knew what mattered.

Today, [PostHog Code](/code) enters beta (but it's much more than middleware). It's an agentic engineering tool that automates the reactive parts of building and growing a product and frames the proactive ones for you to steer. It's also the first public piece of a much bigger bet: a self-driving product.

If you've been following [our AI work](/blog/8-learnings-from-1-year-of-agents-posthog-ai), you probably saw this coming. If not, you might reasonably wonder why we think we can win in a space that has Cursor, Claude Code, Codex, and a new entrant every other Tuesday. This post is the map, how we're thinking about it, and why I think you should give our version of agentic engineering a shot.

## Borrowing a taxonomy from the car people

The Society of Automotive Engineers has had a framework for this since 2014. Six levels of self-driving, from Level 0 (you do everything) to Level 5 (the car does everything, everywhere, in any condition). It's a good framework because it forces specificity about where the machine takes over and where the human is still on the hook. Here's our version, mapped onto product development.

**Level 0 – No automation.** Analytics without AI. Dashboards, filters, raw SQL. Everything you do, you do with your own two hands. This is most of what shipped before 2024.

**Level 1 – Basic data analysis.** An assistant answers a specific, well-formed question. *"What's the trend in signups?" "What's our conversion rate, broken down by plan?"* It falls apart when the question is ambiguous, when no event matches, when several do, or when the query engine can't express what you asked. This was the first version of [PostHog AI](/ai).

**Level 2 – Versatile data analysis.** The assistant handles ambiguity and stitches queries together. *"Which features do users use most?" "Forecast revenue twelve months out."* It falls apart when it lacks product context, or when it shortcuts the reasoning — answering "feature usage" before checking whether feature usage is tracked properly in the first place. PostHog AI [reached this point](/blog/8-learnings-from-1-year-of-agents-posthog-ai) in late 2025.

**Level 3 – Actionable product guidance.** The assistant combines information from many sources and gives real advice grounded in specifics. *"How do we improve our conversion rate?" "Design an experiment to validate our pricing." "Propose a Q4 roadmap."* It falls apart when the advice is generic, or when it ignores the places that actually hold the answer — session replays, GitHub issues, Slack threads, user interviews, chats with a coworker on the way to Chipotle. We are here today, with PostHog AI and the [PostHog MCP](/docs/model-context-protocol).

**Level 4 – Product autonomy.** The agent watches your data, your support tickets, your experiments, your internal comms, the industry news. It proposes work without being asked. This is the self-driving product. This is what every agentic coding tool is supposedly racing toward. And it's the lane we're building in.

**Level 5 – Business autonomy.** *"Just build me an app that generates $1M MRR, please bro, make no mistakes."* We might get here. But do we want to?

The point of the taxonomy isn't to be cute about self-driving cars. It's to make "AI-native" mean something specific instead of a vibe. Most of the industry is stuck at Level 2. The jump to Level 3 and beyond doesn't come from a better model — it comes from giving the agent three things it's usually missing.

## Tools, skills, and signals

Every company trying to build past Level 2 has been shipping roughly half of what the agent actually needs. There are three pieces, and they are not interchangeable.

### Tools: what the agent's hands can do

Tools are agent-friendly descriptions of what your product can do — the interface between the agent and your existing APIs. Without tools, your product is invisible to an agent. It's a house with no doors.

Concretely: when you ask PostHog AI to create a feature flag, it isn't hallucinating SQL. It's calling the same [`create-feature-flag`](/docs/feature-flags/start-here) endpoint that Cursor or Claude Code would hit against our [MCP server](/docs/model-context-protocol). Same tool, different hands.

MCP tool calls against PostHog's MCP server have grown from around 60,000 in October 2025 to roughly 1.26 million in March 2026 — 21× in five months. They come from more than 70 different MCP clients: Claude Code, Cursor, VS Code, Codex, Lovable, Windsurf, Copilot, Gemini CLI, and a long tail of things we didn't know existed a year ago. And zoom out: human-driven AI usage inside PostHog (Max chat) has grown from ~5K to ~15K DAU over the last 90 days, while agent-driven MCP usage is tracking at roughly 60% of that and growing faster. The two curves will cross in 2026. MCP servers, function schemas, OpenAPI specs — they're all [essentially the same shape](/blog/machine-copy-paste-mcp-intro), and nearly every serious SaaS product with a halfway-decent API has shipped one by now.

**So what:** if you haven't shipped an MCP server or a machine-readable API surface, you are invisible in this stack. Tools are table stakes. Ship them.

### Skills: the opinionated layer

Tools let an agent make an API call. [Skills](/handbook/growth/sales/turning-knowledge-into-agent-skills) let an agent make the *right* API call, with the right configuration, in the right order, for the right situation. A skill is an opinionated reference — "how a job should be done at this company" — written for a reader that doesn't have context, doesn't read the room, and will ask the same question 10,000 times.

Picture an agent asked to set up an experiment.

*Without a skill:* it reaches for six different tools, picks the wrong primary metric, forgets to set a minimum detectable effect, and launches something that will never reach significance.

*With a skill:* it reads "how to run an [experiment](/docs/experiments/start-here) at PostHog," follows the prescribed order — hypothesis, primary metric, MDE, exposure, launch — and does the job on the first try.

Here's what the absence of skills looks like in our own data: the single most-called tool on PostHog's MCP server right now is `docs-search`, at roughly 28,000 calls a month. That is every agent, more or less, getting stuck and asking our docs for directions. It's the clearest possible evidence of a skills gap, and we're actively closing it via the [skills store](/docs/llm-analytics/skills-store).

**So what:** every agent hitting `docs-search` is a skill you haven't shipped. Writing a skill feels like writing a doc — and most engineers would rather ship the feature than document it. But a skill *is* the feature, for a non-human user.

### Signals: when work should happen

Signals are indications from a system that work actually needs to happen. A spike in errors. A drop in the checkout funnel. A replay of a user rage-clicking the same button three times before bailing. A support ticket that sounds suspiciously like three others this week. Tools and skills tell the agent *how* to do the work. Signals tell it *when.*

Here's one walked through end to end. A spike in a specific exception fires in [error tracking](/docs/error-tracking/start-here). A [session replay](/docs/session-replay/start-here) shows three users rage-clicking the same button before the crash. That signal triggers a long-running agent. The agent uses a skill to pick the right tools, writes a fix, opens a PR, and verifies the behavior against the same dashboard that fired the signal in the first place.

**So what:** most products have tools. A few have skills. Almost nobody has signals. Signals are the difference between an agent that waits to be asked and one that goes looking for work.

## The loop

String the three together and a pattern falls out.

*Collect data → cluster signals → notify → do work → analyze results → repeat.*

This loop is the difference between Level 3 and Level 4. You can't hand-roll it inside a general-purpose coding agent, because the signals live somewhere else. They live wherever you capture what your users are actually doing. For a lot of companies, that somewhere else is PostHog.

That loop is the self-driving product. Nobody has shipped a canonical version of it. Yet.

## Why vibecoders top out at Level 3

Lovable, Replit, Bolt, v0, and the rest of the vibecoding cohort have proven you can get surprisingly far with just tools and skills. Plug in the right MCP servers, point the agent at a Figma file, and ship a working app before lunch. That's a legitimate Level 3 experience for *building* software. Lovable itself is already one of the top clients hitting our MCP server — even the vibecoders are reaching for PostHog's data layer.

The ceiling is instrumentation. The moment a vibecoded app ships, the agent can't see what happens next — the crash, the rage-click, the silent 8% drop in checkout conversion, the flag you rolled out to 5% of users that regressed LLM latency. Unless someone bolts analytics, error tracking, feature flags, and experiments onto the app after the fact, the agent is blind post-ship.

That work is baked into PostHog by default. A team shipping with the PostHog SDK is already instrumented for [error tracking](/docs/error-tracking/start-here), [session replay](/docs/session-replay/start-here), [feature flags](/docs/feature-flags/start-here), [experiments](/docs/experiments/start-here), and [LLM analytics](/docs/llm-analytics/start-here). In a typical week, PostHog customers generate 100,000+ failed queries (a persistent 3–6% failure rate against roughly 4 million completions), 1.2 to 2.6 million new error-tracking issues, and 12 to 47 million raw exceptions — each one a candidate signal for an agent to act on. Level 4 isn't a better coding agent. It's a coding agent plugged into the layer of signals telling it what to do next.

## Which brings us to PostHog Code

We've been sitting on a weirdly complete set of ingredients for this:

- **Data** — years of it. [Errors](/docs/error-tracking/start-here), [session replays](/docs/session-replay/start-here), [LLM traces](/docs/llm-analytics/start-here), [experiments](/docs/experiments/start-here), [feature flags](/docs/feature-flags/start-here), revenue — the event stream of what real users are actually doing.
- **Tooling** — ClickHouse for storing and querying events, query engines for experiments, a [data warehouse](/docs/data-warehouse/start-here) for modeling, Temporal for long-running jobs.
- **Sandboxes** — cloud agents for running code safely without blowing up production.
- **Devtools** — a growing catalog of the things product engineers actually reach for.

The raw material is already sitting there, mostly in PostHog itself. Almost none of it turns into a notification, a diagnosis, or a PR automatically. It sits there, waiting for a human to notice.

PostHog Code is the first public piece. It's an agentic engineering tool that sits on top of your product data and does two things.

**First, it ships PRs automatically for mechanical decisions.** Error tracking shows a spike in a specific exception; the agent reads the stack trace, finds the regression, writes the fix, and verifies the behavior in [session replay](/docs/session-replay/start-here). Nothing subjective in the loop. Faster than you. Cost of getting it slightly wrong: a one-line revert.

**Second, it frames bounded decisions so you can actually decide.** Ten support tickets about a confusing checkout flow? The agent does the diligence — pulls the [replays](/docs/session-replay/start-here), quantifies the drop-off, drafts the options — and hands the decision to you. Redesign the flow, add a tooltip, kill the feature, or decide it isn't worth working on at all. You still make the call. The legwork is already done.

Practically, that means a prioritized to-do list instead of a noisy one, and automatic PRs for the stuff that doesn't need a meeting. You can switch between Claude Code and Codex from a single interface, plug in your MCP servers (Attio, Canva, Atlassian, Notion, Monday), and pull context from Linear, Zendesk, and GitHub. One subscription, one context layer, both of the big agentic harnesses.

## The workbench, and the vehicle it's being built in

The long-term vision is self-driving for your product. The agent runs in the background, watches your data, fixes what's fixable, frames what needs a judgement call, and you spend your time on the parts of the business that need a human.

We're not quite there yet. The current version of PostHog Code is more of a workbench. It's useful on its own, and — importantly — it's ahead of general-purpose agents on the one axis we care about: product context comes pre-loaded. You don't have to tab-switch, copy-paste, or upload a screenshot of your own product into the agent chat like a lunatic. Explore your data, build something, ship it, control the blast radius, and analyze the results, all from a single interface.

A workbench isn't the destination. It's the shop we're building the vehicle in.

The self-driving version of this product will be built on data that other companies shipping AI IDEs simply don't have. Error trends, web analytics, session replay summaries, acquisition and onboarding funnels, experiments, LLM traces, the event stream of how real users behave. [PostHog has been processing that for years.](/ai)

Every month, the set of decisions we can safely move from bounded into mechanical gets a little bigger. Skills get better. Data gets richer. Feedback loops get tighter. The PRs we ship automatically get more impressive by the week.

The trade-off: *"scoped to your product"* means we aren't going to run your whole business and generate $1M MRR while you take a nap. Maybe someday. But if fully autonomous CEO mode is what you wanted, you probably wouldn't be reading a product-engineering blog.

## TL;DR

Three things an agent needs from your product: **tools** (table stakes), **skills** (the opinionated layer), and **signals** (the trigger that closes the loop). String all three together and you get a self-driving product.

[PostHog Code](/code) is the workbench we're building it in. It ships PRs for the stuff that's obviously broken and frames the judgement calls for you to steer.
